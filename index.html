Extinction: Caused By Man?

The rise of extinction deniers




This illustration shows several animals made extinct by mankind. Animals such as the Dodo, the Mamo, the Passenger Pigeon, and the Tasmanian Tiger have been gone extinct due to human-caused factors such as overhunting or extermination.



What does extinction mean?

Extinction is when every individual of an animal or plant species dies out. Extinction has become a major crisis especially now in history as species are dying out at alarming rates. Humans have encouraged this heightened rate of extinction by “hunting, overharvesting, introducing invasive species to the wild, polluting, and changing wetlands and forests to croplands and urban areas” (National Geographic).The main concern these days is habitat destruction, which is changing animal’s habitats into human habitats and destroying the animal’s homes. As humans encroach on animal’s lands, the animals are forced into smaller and smaller areas or are forced to come face to face with humans. We see that if you put animals (including humans) in a habitat that is shrinking, they will become more and more desperate to escape as the habitat gets closer and closer to being too small and the animal gets closer and closer to being crushed. This has been shown in many movies including Star Wars with the trash compactor scene (Episode 4, A New Hope). As the walls of the trash compactor move in, Han, Luke, and Leia become increasingly desperate to get out and Luke’s calls for help become higher pitched and convey more urgency. Situations like this, but in the real world, often end up with scared animals, sometimes large animals running amok, terrified, in urban areas. ScienceNews states that “Only 3 percent of Earth’s land hasn’t been marred by humans” and yet some people still do not believe that humans are pushing species to extinction. ScienceNews also noted that “Thanks to human activities, the rate of global species extinctions is tens to hundreds of times faster than the average extinction rate was over the last 10 million years.”

How do scientists decide if a species is extinct?

The International Union for the Conservation of Nature (IUCN) is the agency that monitors the populations of endangered animals on a global scale. This agency has a large variety of sources it uses to get information about these animal populations. They work with conservationists, biologists, and statisticians to try to get an idea of how each species is doing. Using many different techniques, technology, and teams of people, scientists record information about species they are concerned about. A species whose population drops by 70% in a decade is listed as endangered. A species whose population decreases by 90% in a decade is listed as critically endangered.



What is the controversy behind extinction?

One of the reasons people deny extinction is that there are a very low confirmed number of extinctions every year. The last 40 years have seen an average extinction rate of about 2 species per year, and of those, not all are declared extinct on the IUCN Red List. They have been declared extinct (even by IUCN itself) but the list has not yet been updated. What does science suggest about “extinction deniers”?

In an interview with Dr. Tim Wootton, a professor in the department of ecology and evolution at the University of Chicago He stated that “There have been measurements of extinction rates in recent times based on historical records and this has been translated into an estimated rate of extinction. And then compared to invertebrates from the fossil record.” He also stated that. ”Evidence is that rates of extinction in the current period are as high as some of the larger mass extinction rates.” And noted that “Unlike some of the past events we don’t have meteors crashing to the earth or things like that.” We do have a sense of why a lot of documented extinctions have occurred. Through habitat loss and overhunting.

How do humans cause extinction?

Humans over the years have started to become a leading cause of extinction. We have destroyed land, hunted animals, and many more things. Over many studies over time, we can see that humans have reshaped 75% of land on earth and many parts of the ocean, for our own benefit and not thinking of the animals. Overall about 1 million plants and animals have gone extinct, many within just decades. Humans also do very little for conservation to help the endangered animals. The New York Times states “With the human population passing 7 billion, activities like farming, logging, poaching, fishing and mining are altering the natural world at a rate “unprecedented in human history.” “, just going farther into depth of how much humans have done to raise the extinction rates every day. There are many reasons that humans would doubt that they are causing extinction, but the main one is disinformation. Recently, multiple sources have been put out that state that extinction rates are normal and that there is no reason to be concerned about this widespread death. Extinction deniers also point out that there are a very low level of confirmed extinct species, but the simple fact is that it is very hard to confirm a species as extinct. All it takes is 1 picture to say that the animal is not extinct, but it takes days of surveying, searching, and investigating to declare a species as extinct. This is important because extinction is a double edged sword. If one species goes extinct, it often leads to more species going extinct and sometimes, in extreme cases, it can lead to the extinction of an entire ecosystem. Every extinct animal is another animal we cannot learn from and another ecosystem that is getting closer to collapse. We must realize this as the horror that it is and if we don’t our grandchildren or their grandchildren might live in a world with no rhinos, no elephants, no wolves, and none of the other iconic animals that we live with today.
